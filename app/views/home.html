The Ignored

The unkempt, scruffy man parading around a cardboard sign in a silent plea is enough for a long hard look at Mr. Lincoln in the wallet. The money should go to a worthy cause, a vector of benevolence to one in need. Hmmm, Abe would be better spent on the daily cappuccino instead of some homeless guy’s “food” (probably drugs). Besides, you need the caffeine. When the TV chances upon the news segment following the family under the bridge, you quickly change the channel; surely it’s more fun to watch the Splash Brothers rain threes than worry about those who, when it actually rains, shelter under the trees. The general consensus about homelessness, a dire issue deserving attention, is betrayed by the paradoxical reality: no one really cares.

What most don’t realize is that many homeless face discrimination that most would never encounter. A UC Berkeley homeless study breaks down the anti-homeless laws in the 58 most populous California cities into four categories: (1) standing, sitting, and resting in public places; (2) sleeping, camping, and lodging in public places, including in vehicles; (3) begging and panhandling; and (4) food sharing. Quoted from that study: “All 58 most populous California cities have at least one municipal code restricting daytime activities like standing, sitting, and resting.” San Francisco, Palo Alto, and Santa Cruz are just a few of the cities that have passed Sit-Lie Ordinances, laws that restrict sitting or lying down in public places. Even in our own San Jose, impromptu homeless feedings are banned due to “health concerns”.
